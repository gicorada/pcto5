Elon Musk

Elon Musk is a South African-born entrepreneur and innovator who has become a household name in the tech industry. He is known for his role as CEO and co-founder of several companies, including Tesla, SpaceX, Neuralink, and The Boring Company.

Elon Musk is a business magnate, well known because of his technology and engineering projects. He founded many companies, such as The Boring Company (that builds underground transportation systems), Tesla, Neuralink and many others. Some months ago, he also bought Twitter for $44 billion. He is a very criticized person, because he has made controversial statements on politics and technology, and he also has made unscientific and misleading statements. Musk was born in South Africa, and he later moved to the United States to attend the University of Pennsylvania. After completing his studies, he started some of his projects. His major projects are PayPal (sold to eBay), Zip2 (which provided online city guide services, sold to Compaq), SpaceX, Tesla, Neuralink. He is also involved in other activities, such as HyperLoop and OpenAI. Musk's success in these projects has made him one of the wealthiest people in the world.

Until 2013, Elon Musk's children all attended the exclusive Mirman school of excellence for gifted children based in Los Angeles, but Musk was disappointed and frustrated by the structure and teachings of the school, although it is not known why, to be honest, he never disclosed the details. In 2014 he decided to found his own version of the school together with Joshua Daun, a teacher among the managers of the Mirman school and other teachers of the highest level who shared his pedagogical ideas. Initially the school was reserved exclusively for Elon's children and was organized inside a SpaceX conference room, then it gradually expanded to children of relevant figures of Musk's companies and now hosts about 40 boys and girls between 8 and 14 years, and there is also a rather obscure and incomprehensible where it seems that your child can apply, with online questions about the colonization of planets and problems on which the boy must express himself. The selection process, as well as the parameters used, is completely mysterious and arbitrary, we read around employees of SpaceX who can't even get information about the school, despite the fact that in their contract with the company, Ad Astra was mentioned.

What Makes Elon Musk's School Different Everything else is teaching practice. He develops students fascinated by complexity and solving the unknown. It is annually based on student's learning path. It applies lessons learned new programs from every project, workshop and conversation next step. Often the school system is strict and more suitable for the system compares students. Usually, the school system is rigid and more focussed on system than on the student. Ad Astra is changing that status quo by creating a student-centered philosophy, which value personal skills, praise curiosity, encourage problem solving and critical thinking. Curiosity is one of the most important human qualities. Elon Musk uses this quality to encourage independent and inquiry-based learning, harnessing students' natural curiosity to realign their relationship to the unknown and unlock their limitless potential.

It does not have a social profile, and above all, it does not differentiate between school grades, as first graders are in the same class as third graders."You need to advance kids to the same grade in the same time, like an assembly line." This is one of the things he said in an interview in which he reveals the purpose of this exclusive school, which today has 31 pupils, including his 5 children. As a child, Elon hated school, hated it so much that he was terrified of going to it. In an interview, he says that when he was at his school in Pretoria, South Africa, he was bullied several times. His comrades had pushed him down the stairwell and in another case he was beaten up to hospital. To date, Astra has only three teachers who aim to satisfy the abilities of each individual student and not to make them follow an arbitration program, but in a constructive way and thus developing problem solving.
